PJM 5-Bus Example — Notes
=========================

Generators
----------
The three generators are taken directly from the IonQ/ORNL unit commitment paper
(arXiv:2505.00145, Table I) and placed on the PJM 5-bus network topology from
MATPOWER case5.m:

  Unit 0 @ Bus 1: p_min=100 MW, p_max=600 MW, cost b=$10/MWh
  Unit 1 @ Bus 3: p_min=100 MW, p_max=400 MW, cost b=$8/MWh
  Unit 2 @ Bus 5: p_min= 50 MW, p_max=200 MW, cost b=$6/MWh

The original paper uses quadratic costs F(p) = a*p^2 + b*p + c. The DCOPF
framework only supports linear costs, so only the incremental term b is used
here. The fixed no-load cost c and efficiency curve term a are ignored.

Economic Dispatch vs Unit Commitment
-------------------------------------
pjm5_cost.py runs economic dispatch, not unit commitment. The difference:

- Economic dispatch (what this script does): all generators are assumed to be
  ON at every time step. The solver only decides how much each one produces to
  meet demand at minimum cost, subject to line flow limits and generator limits.

- Unit commitment (what the uc_quantum_tutorial notebook does): the solver also
  decides which generators to turn ON or OFF at each time step using binary
  variables. This adds startup/shutdown costs and minimum up/down time constraints.
  The combinatorial nature of the binary decisions is what makes it interesting
  for quantum optimization.

Because all generators are always ON here, the p_min constraints are always
enforced. Unit 2 at Bus 5 is the cheapest generator ($6/MWh) and will be
dispatched to its 200 MW ceiling in most hours. This frequently saturates the
line 4-5 limit (240 MW), creating congestion and LMP separation between those
two buses — which is the main economic phenomenon this network illustrates.

To do proper unit commitment on this network, the binary on/off decisions and
quadratic costs from the paper would need to be added on top of the DCOPF layer.
